Module 01

Why Ethics Matter in Data Science

LO → Explain why ethics are important in data science.

Data scientists wield enormous power. The models you build and the data you handle can affect hiring decisions, loan approvals, criminal sentencing, healthcare access, and more. Ethics are the guardrails that ensure this power is used responsibly.

Key Idea: Unlike physical engineering (bridges, buildings), the consequences of unethical data science can be invisible—people may never know an algorithm denied them an opportunity.

Ethics in data science matter because of scale (one model can affect millions), opacity (decisions are often hidden inside code), and permanence (data and models persist long after they're created).

Real-World Case

In 2018, Amazon scrapped an internal AI recruiting tool after discovering it systematically penalized résumés that contained the word "women's" (e.g., "women's chess club"). The model had learned historical hiring bias from a decade of predominantly male applicant data.

Data scientists have a duty of care to consider not just whether their model is accurate, but whether it is fair, transparent, and aligned with human values.

Anonymizing Datasets

LO → Define ways to protect privacy by anonymizing datasets.

Anonymization is the process of transforming data so that individuals cannot be identified. This is a cornerstone of ethical data handling.

🔀 Data Masking
Replacing sensitive values with fictional but realistic substitutes (e.g., replacing real names with fake ones).

📊 Aggregation
Reporting data in groups or summaries rather than individual records (e.g., average salary by department instead of per person).

🎲 K-Anonymity
Ensuring each record is indistinguishable from at least k−1 other records on quasi-identifiers (like age, zip code, gender).
tap to flip
📉 Generalization
Reducing precision of data (e.g., replacing exact age "27" with a range "25–30", or full zip code with first 3 digits).
tap to flip
🔊 Differential Privacy
Adding calibrated random noise to query results so that no single individual's data significantly affects the output. Used by the U.S. Census Bureau and Apple.
tap to flip
✂️ Data Suppression
Removing certain fields or records entirely when they could identify individuals (e.g., removing names, SSNs, or rare conditions from a health dataset).
tap to flip
Watch out: Simply removing names is often not enough. Research has shown that 87% of the U.S. population can be uniquely identified by just zip code, birth date, and sex (Sweeney, 2000).

Module 03

Algorithmic Bias

LO → Recognize cases of algorithmic bias.

Algorithmic bias occurs when a computer system produces results that are systematically unfair to certain groups of people. This is not always intentional—it often emerges from biased training data, flawed feature selection, or unexamined assumptions.

Common sources of bias:

Sources of Algorithmic Bias

Historical bias — Training data reflects past discrimination (e.g., hiring data from eras with fewer women in tech).

Representation bias — Some groups are underrepresented in training data (e.g., medical imaging datasets with few non-white patients).

Measurement bias — The way data is collected systematically distorts outcomes (e.g., using arrest records as a proxy for crime rates).

Aggregation bias — Assuming one model fits all populations when subgroups behave differently.

Case Studies to Know

🏥 Healthcare algorithm: A widely used health system allocated care based on healthcare spending. Because Black patients historically had less access to care (and thus lower spending), the algorithm assigned them lower risk scores—even when they were sicker.

⚖️ COMPAS recidivism tool: A criminal sentencing tool was found to produce higher false-positive rates for Black defendants than white defendants when predicting re-offense risk.

📷 Facial recognition: Commercial systems showed significantly higher error rates for darker-skinned women compared to lighter-skinned men.

Module 04

Consent in Data Science

LO → Recognize issues of consent in data science.

Informed consent means that individuals knowingly and voluntarily agree to the collection and use of their data, with a clear understanding of what data is collected, how it will be used, and what risks are involved.

The Consent Problem: Most "consent" today happens through lengthy Terms of Service agreements that almost nobody reads. A 2017 Deloitte survey found that 91% of people accept ToS without reading them.

Common consent issues in data science:

Red Flags

🔄 Secondary use: Data collected for one purpose is repurposed for another without new consent (e.g., fitness tracker data sold to insurance companies).

🕸️ Web scraping: Collecting publicly posted data and using it in ways the poster never intended (e.g., scraping social media photos to train facial recognition).

👶 Vulnerable populations: Children, elderly people, or those with limited digital literacy may not fully understand what they are consenting to.

🔗 Data linkage: Combining datasets can reveal information about individuals who never consented to that combined analysis.

Module 05

Environmental Impact of Big Data

LO → Recognize environmental impacts of big data.

The computational infrastructure behind data science—data centers, GPU clusters, network equipment—has a significant and growing environmental footprint.

Key Environmental Concerns

⚡ Energy consumption: Training a single large language model can consume as much energy as five cars over their entire lifetimes (Strubell et al., 2019). Data centers account for roughly 1–2% of global electricity use.

💧 Water usage: Data centers use massive amounts of water for cooling. A single large data center can use millions of gallons of water per day.

🗑️ E-waste: Rapid hardware turnover (GPUs, servers) contributes to electronic waste as equipment is replaced every 3–5 years.

🌍 Carbon footprint: The carbon cost depends on where the data center is located and the regional energy mix (coal vs. renewables).

Ethical question: Is the societal benefit of a given model or analysis worth its environmental cost? Data scientists should consider whether a simpler, less resource-intensive approach could achieve similar results.

Common Misuses of Statistics

LO → Summarize common misuses of statistics.

Statistics can be a powerful tool for truth—or for deception. Understanding common misuses helps you both avoid mistakes and spot manipulation.

🔗 Confusing Correlation with Causation
Just because two variables move together doesn't mean one causes the other. Ice cream sales and drowning rates both rise in summer—but ice cream doesn't cause drowning.
tap to flip
🍒 Cherry-Picking Data
Selectively choosing data points or time ranges that support a predetermined conclusion while ignoring contradictory evidence.
tap to flip
📐 Misleading Averages
Using mean vs. median strategically. A CEO earning $10M and 9 workers earning $50K each yields a "mean salary" of ~$1M—a wildly misleading summary.
tap to flip
🔍 P-Hacking
Running many statistical tests and only reporting the ones that show significant results, dramatically increasing the chance of false positives.
tap to flip
👥 Sampling Bias
Drawing conclusions from a non-representative sample (e.g., surveying only college students about "all adults" or using opt-in online polls).
tap to flip
🪄 Simpson's Paradox
A trend that appears in subgroups of data reverses when the groups are combined. Ignoring this can lead to completely wrong conclusions.
tap to flip

Critiquing Misleading Graphs

LO → Critique misleading graphs.

Graphs are powerful because people trust visual information. That trust can be exploited through techniques that distort perception without technically "lying."

Common tricks to watch for:

Misleading Techniques

📏 Truncated Y-axis: Starting the y-axis at a value other than zero exaggerates differences between bars or data points.

↔️ Inconsistent scales: Using different scales on dual axes or between compared charts to distort comparisons.

🗺️ Manipulated aspect ratio: Stretching or compressing a chart to make trends appear steeper or flatter.

🎨 3D effects: Adding unnecessary 3D rendering that makes it hard to accurately read values.

📊 Area/volume tricks: Doubling the radius of a circle to show "2×" actually increases the area by 4×.

Can you spot what's misleading?

Truncated Y-axis! The y-axis starts at $3.9M instead of $0. The actual revenue increased from $4.0M to $4.5M — a modest 12.5% growth. But by zooming in on just the $3.9M–$4.5M range, the chart makes Q4's bar appear roughly 3.5× taller than Q1's, dramatically exaggerating the growth and making it look like revenue nearly quadrupled.

Module 08

Confidentiality

LO → Define confidential.
Confidential
Information that is intended to be kept secret and is shared only with authorized individuals. Confidentiality is the obligation to protect this information from unauthorized access or disclosure.

In data science, confidentiality applies to many types of information: personal health records (protected by HIPAA), financial data, trade secrets, proprietary algorithms, and personally identifiable information (PII).

Confidentiality ≠ Privacy. Privacy is the individual's right to control their own information. Confidentiality is the obligation placed on those who receive that information to protect it. You can have privacy without a confidentiality agreement, and you can have a confidentiality agreement even when the information isn't "private" in the traditional sense.

Types of Confidential Data

🏥 Protected Health Information (PHI): Medical records, diagnoses, treatment plans

💳 Financial PII: Credit card numbers, bank accounts, credit scores

🏢 Proprietary business data: Trade secrets, unreleased product info, internal analytics

🔐 Credentials: Passwords, API keys, encryption keys

Protective Precautions

LO → Identify precautions that protect individuals and organizations.

Protecting data requires a layered approach spanning technical controls, organizational policies, and individual practices.

Technical Precautions

🔒 Encryption: Encrypting data at rest and in transit so intercepted data is unreadable.

🔑 Access controls: Role-based access ensures people only see data they need for their job (principle of least privilege).

📋 Audit logs: Recording who accessed what data and when, enabling accountability.

🛡️ Firewalls & network security: Preventing unauthorized external access to data stores.

Organizational Precautions

📜 Data governance policies: Formal rules about data collection, storage, retention, and deletion.

🎓 Ethics training: Ensuring all team members understand their responsibilities.

🧪 Ethics review boards (IRBs): Independent review of data projects involving human subjects.

📅 Data retention limits: Deleting data after its intended use period ends rather than hoarding it indefinitely.

Individual Precautions

🔐 Strong passwords & MFA: Multi-factor authentication for sensitive systems.

📵 Clean desk policy: Not leaving sensitive data visible on screens or in physical form.

🚫 Avoiding data on personal devices: Keeping organizational data off personal laptops and phones.

Module 10

Data Privacy Tradeoffs

LO → Discuss data privacy tradeoffs.

Privacy is rarely absolute. In practice, data science involves navigating tensions between competing values. Understanding these tradeoffs is more useful than demanding absolute privacy or unlimited data access.

Common Tradeoffs

🔬 Privacy vs. Research Utility: More anonymization protects individuals but may reduce the usefulness of data for medical research, public health, and scientific discovery.

🛡️ Privacy vs. Security: Surveillance systems can improve public safety but at the cost of personal freedom and privacy (e.g., facial recognition in public spaces).

📱 Privacy vs. Convenience: Personalized recommendations (Netflix, Spotify, Maps) require sharing behavioral data. More privacy means less personalization.

💰 Privacy vs. Free Services: Many "free" platforms (social media, email, search) are funded by advertising based on user data. Strict privacy limits could change this business model.

🏥 Privacy vs. Public Health: Contact tracing during pandemics requires location data sharing. Too much privacy protection can hamper disease control.

Key Insight: The goal is not to eliminate tradeoffs but to make them explicit and informed. Users should understand what they're giving up, and organizations should minimize data collection to only what's necessary (data minimization principle).

Principles of Ethical Machine Learning

LO → List principles of ethical machine learning.

As ML systems take on increasingly consequential roles, the data science community has developed a set of core principles to guide ethical development:

⚖️ Fairness
ML systems should treat all individuals and groups equitably. Test for and mitigate bias across demographic groups. No group should be systematically disadvantaged.

🔍 Transparency
Stakeholders should be able to understand how and why a model makes decisions. Document data sources, model architecture, training processes, and known limitations.
tap to flip
📋 Accountability
Clear lines of responsibility must exist. When an ML system causes harm, there should be identifiable people or organizations responsible for remediation.
tap to flip
🔐 Privacy
Respect user data rights. Collect only necessary data, protect it rigorously, give users control over their information, and comply with regulations (GDPR, CCPA).
tap to flip
🛡️ Safety & Reliability
ML systems should perform reliably under expected conditions and fail gracefully. Rigorous testing, monitoring, and the ability to override or shut down systems is essential.

Humans should remain in the loop for high-stakes decisions. ML should augment human judgment, not replace it entirely, especially in domains like healthcare, criminal justice, and hiring.

Black Box Models

LO → Define black box model.
Black Box Model
A machine learning model whose internal logic and decision-making process is too complex or opaque for humans to understand. You can see the inputs and outputs, but not how or why it reached its conclusion.

Examples of black box models: deep neural networks, large ensemble methods, and large language models. These can have millions or billions of parameters, making it impossible to trace the exact reasoning behind any single prediction.

Why black boxes are an ethical concern:

The Problem

If you can't explain why a model denied someone a loan, flagged someone as a security threat, or recommended a medical treatment, you can't verify whether the decision was fair, catch errors, or enable meaningful appeal.

Approaches to address this:

Solutions

🔎 Explainability tools (XAI): Techniques like LIME and SHAP that approximate explanations for individual predictions.

📐 Interpretable alternatives: Using simpler models (decision trees, logistic regression) where possible, especially in high-stakes domains.

📋 "Right to explanation": The EU's GDPR gives individuals the right to meaningful information about the logic of automated decisions that significantly affect them.

Module 13

Ethical Concerns in ML Applications

LO → Identify ethical concerns in machine learning applications.

Machine learning is being deployed in increasingly sensitive domains. Each application raises distinct ethical questions.

High-Stakes ML Applications & Their Concerns

⚖️ Criminal justice (predictive policing, sentencing): Risk of reinforcing racial biases, lack of transparency, and questionable accuracy affecting people's liberty.

🏥 Healthcare (diagnosis, treatment recommendations): Liability for incorrect diagnoses, bias in training data (underrepresentation of minorities), and patient consent for AI-assisted decisions.

🏦 Financial services (credit scoring, fraud detection): Potential for discriminatory lending, use of proxy variables that correlate with protected characteristics.

📹 Surveillance (facial recognition, behavior prediction): Mass surveillance concerns, chilling effects on free speech and assembly, disproportionate impact on marginalized communities.

🤖 Autonomous systems (self-driving cars, drones): Moral decisions in unavoidable accidents, liability questions, and safety assurance.

💼 Hiring & HR (résumé screening, performance prediction): Bias against certain demographics, lack of transparency in rejection decisions, reduced human judgment in important life decisions.

Recurring themes: Across all these domains, the core ethical concerns often come back to fairness, transparency, accountability, consent, and human oversight—the same principles from Module 11.
